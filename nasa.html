Moon Buddy

An AR System for Lunar Expeditions

Moon Buddy is a voice-controlled, heads-up augmented reality display designed to assist astronauts on future lunar exploration missions. I played a core part in user research, prototyping, and usability testing.

Research

Preliminary Research How to begin work in an unfamiliar field with high levels of complexity? To gain an understanding of a technical problem space I was largely unfamiliar with, I looked into three major areas when conducting prelimary research: 1. Previous SUITS publications: Although the SUITS challenge is different every year, other teams had a lot of valuable research and findings to provide. 2. NASA AR Research Projects: NASA and their partners have been exploring and testing many AR-based applications to assist with a variety of space-related tasks. 3. Apollo Transcripts: Given that Apollo was the last time NASA sent astronauts to the moon, the audio transcripts for each mission were a treasure trove of data for identifying real user pain points in an environment far, far away. User Interview with Jay Apt Pain Points Based on this research, Anita and I were able to determine the primary pain points, which ultimately shaped our core design goals. 1. Pain Point: Poor Mobility in Spacesuit Goal: MoonBuddy must be operable while users are wearing EVA gloves and while executing dexterous tasks. 2. Pain Point: Limited Vision from Helmet + Harsh Lighting COnditions Goal: MoonBuddy must provide environmental awareness of hazardous regions and be unobtrusive to astronaut view. 3. Pain Point: Limited Communication with MCC Goal: MoonBuddy must give both the MCC and astronauts the autonomy to modify tasks and mission procedure at any point in the EVA. 4. Pain Point: Cognitive Overload Goal: MoonBuddy must provide information in a simple manner and limit information to show only what is needed. Future space exploration missions will have an increased need for equipping astronauts with the autonomy-enabling tools they need for the elevated demands of lunar surface exploration and extreme terrestrial access.

NASA has invested in AR as a solution in the form of JARVIS, a Joint Augmented Reality Visual Informatics System that is integrated directly into the spacesuit.

To meet NASA's open challenge to design an AR information display for lunar exploration, our team developed MoonBuddy, a voice-based AR application that integrates navigation, task assistance, documentation tools, and hazard avoidance through geometry-aware surface highlighting.

Design Explorations Our design team continously iterated our designs through weekly feedback sessions with the rest of our team and our mentor from NASA. - snapshots - (Month Year) Feature Final Design Below is the final design on the HoloLens2 that our team tested with multiple stakeholders at Johnson Space Center in June 2022. Design System 1. Onboarding Users are onboarded through a hybrid method to get used to the voice commands and visual layout. 2. Navigation Users can say "Go to Point X" for visual guidance to their next location. 3. Science Sampling Users are walked through the sampling process for the specific types of samples they've found at their destination site. 4. Lunar Search and Rescue Users can recieve alerts about crew members in distress and respond quickly and efficiently. 5. Hazard Avoidance Users can utilize the spatial mapping feature to easily detect obstacles such as rocks and craters. User Testing Results We were able to conduct 2 user testing sessions at the JSC Rock Yard under simulated lunar lighting conditions btween 7pm and midnight. Participants went through a 45-minute training before preceding to the experiemental session, where they were tasked to navigate to 3 different sampling sites and document rock samples. A LSAR message was sent at a random time within the testing session, and we concluded with a short debrief to gather user feedback. Users appreciated... 1. The simple voice commands and guided nature of MoonBuddy, especially during the more complicated geological sampling tasks. 2. The surface edge detection feature that allowed them to avoid rocks in the darkness. 3. The intuitive and easy to use UI Users wished that... 1. AR navigation could be anchored to the real world to add superimposed landmarks in an environment that lacks them. 2. There was adapative brightness for UI elements to balance visibility. Reflection While this was our team's first and last year working on this project, we saw a lot of opportunities for further refinement in the future, especially through more experimentation with AR's unique ability to directly interact and impact an astronaut's immediate surroundings.
